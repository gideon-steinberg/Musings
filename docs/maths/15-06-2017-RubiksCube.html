Solving a Rubik's Cube is not magic

This is a rubik's cube:

If you cannot solve this then watching someone taking a rubik's cube and solving it seems like magic.
How can someone take a random scramble and put it back together in a neat order?

I can solve a rubik's cube in around a minute.
It is actually really easy. It kinda gets boring once you learn how to solve it.

There are 43,252,003,274,489,856,000 number of states a rubik's cube can be in.
There is NO way to be able to solve it unless you know what you are doing.
One of these 43 quintillion states is the solved state. Your goal is to take one of these 43 quintillion states and move it to a specific one. This seems impossible but as I mentioned it becomes routine.

Properties of a Rubik's rube

The first thing to do to learn to solve a rubik's cube is to understand it's properties. I will outline them here.
If you want the rubik's cube to stay a mystery stop reading :)

There are three types of pieces. They are:
  • The center pieces with one face
  • The edge pieces with two faces
  • The corner pieces with three faces
As should be obvious when you had a little play with one (or think about it) the pieces can only occupy specific slots based on what type they are.

The center pieces are not part of the puzzle. No seriously, you cannot move them. You can rotate them (which correspondes to the 6 moves you can make) but they do not move.
A rubik's cube solver is moving the edge and corner pieces in relation to the centers.

The edge pieces have 12 spots they can occupy and two orientations they can be in.
The corner pieces have 8 spots they can occupy and three orientations they can be in.

This type of puzzle is known as group theory in mathematics.
I won't go into the specifics but they is a LOT of mathematics research into group theory.
Essentially each move and set of moves transforms the cube from one group to another and you can model it in maths.

How do you solve it?

Simple, you build it step by step.
  1. Choose a colour to start with
  2. Move the 4 edge pieces of that colour in the correct spots
  3. Move the 4 corner pieces of that colour in the correct spots completing the first layer
  4. Move the 4 edge pieces in the second layout into the correct spots completing the second layer
  5. Orient the 4 edge pieces correctly on the last layer
  6. Position the 4 corner pieces in the correct place on the last layer
  7. Orient the 4 corner pieces correctly on the last layer
  8. Position the 4 edge pieces in the correct place on the last layer
Each step is done without destroying your progress from before.
You need to learn an algorithm (or series of algorithms) to move specific pieces without wrecking your hard work.

Step 2 and step 3 can be solved intuatively given some time but the rest require thought (or looking it up).
Step 2 is often known as "Cross" as you are making a cross.
Step 3 and step 4 can be done together in something called F2L (First two layers). This is just faster than doing them by themselves :)
Step 5 and 7 are known as OLL (orienting the last layer) which can be done as one step.
Step 6 and 8 are known as PLL (permuting the last layer) which can be done as one step.
Step 5,6,7 and 8 can be done in any order and they are done in different ways. The way I have given is just how I do it.

I don't do full OLL and full PLL. OLL has 57 distint cases and PLL has 21 distint cases and you need to memorise way too many algorithms.
I do two step OLL and 2 step PLL (in the order given above) as I only need to know 4 algorithms and it is pretty simple.

If you want to learn to solve a rubik's cube there are some resources below that explain exactly how to solve one.
Remember that you need to learn the muscle memory for these algorithms. I don't think about it anymore, I just do the algorithm to move pieces without thinking about it. I mainly think about "Where are those pieces again?".
There are also many other ways to solve it but they generally solve it step by step with specific algorithms.
This is called the beginners method.
